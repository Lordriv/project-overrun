1. Vision
STATUS: LOCKED
	

1.1 Pitch
OVERRUN is a co-op extraction roguelite for 1–4 players, built in Godot. A squad drops into a broken world, fights escalating waves of void creatures, unlocks tech between waves to grow absurd builds, and decides when to extract — or how deep to push before they wipe.
1.2 Run shape
Runs target ~20 minutes to complete a map's primary objective, but squads can stay indefinitely past completion for more loot, more tech, and harder waves. The only loss state is squad wipe, which costs all run loot. Characters persist across runs in a DnD-style fashion — bound weapons, attachments, and cosmetics carry over — but the loot inside any single run is temporary.
1.3 Emotional loop
Find weird loot → discover a synergy → push an absurd build as far as it'll go → lose it spectacularly → queue up again. The build is the reward, not the loot. Loot is sandbox material for build experimentation.
1.4 Tone & aesthetic
Neon-sci-fi apocalypse blended with industrial punk and cosmic ruin. Broken environments, empty halls, collapsed architecture. Scale is deliberately oppressive — the squad should feel small. Void creatures crawl through the world. The hero image is four dwarves silhouetted against a wasteland.
1.5 Co-op philosophy
Generous. Shared loot, revives, squad-lives-or-dies-together — until the very last moment. Extraction is the one mechanic where the squad's collective fate dissolves into individual outcomes. The squad is a composition of distinct builds, not four clones. Tech and augments naturally pull squadmates into different roles every run — not by class declaration, but by what their builds become.


2. Core Gameplay Loop
STATUS: LOCKED
	

2.1 Loop overview
OVERRUN is wave-defense with terminal-driven build progression. The squad drops onto a themed map, holds against escalating waves, and unlocks tech augments at terminals between waves. Each map is an archetype that shapes which kinds of builds are possible.
The full cycle:
1. Drop in. Squad walks 20–30s to a fixed anchor.
2. Wave 1 begins at the anchor.
3. Wave ends. One terminal somewhere on the map becomes active.
4. Squad navigates to active terminal (may have scouted its location during the breather).
5. At the terminal, each player independently sees their own 3 augment options. Each picks one.
6. Next wave begins. Loop repeats.
7. After ~10 cycles (~20 min), Mission Control announces the tech-data threshold has been reached. Squad can extract or push deeper indefinitely.
2.2 Drop-in (first 30 seconds)
Tense breather. The squad lands together, walks toward a fixed anchor point, and the environment sells the world's scale and decay before any combat starts. Wind, distant machine sounds, the suggestion of being watched. By the time they reach the anchor, they should feel small.
The anchor is a known designed defensive position — not chosen, not random. Wave 1 begins here. The squad's extraction pods are also visible from drop-in (see Section 3) — players know where home is from minute one.
2.3 The terminal loop (between waves)
2.3.1 Terminal discovery
Multiple terminals exist on every map. Inactive terminals are visible if a player walks near them and can be discovered during the drop-in walk and during between-wave breathers. The next active terminal is not telegraphed until the previous wave ends.
This means scouting between waves is speculation, not certainty. A squad that's found three terminals has three possible directions for the next wave; only one will light up. Knowledge of map geography becomes a meta-resource.
2.3.2 Terminal activation
When a wave ends, exactly one terminal becomes active. The squad converges on it. If they've scouted, they already know where it is and how to get there. If not, they're scrambling.
2.3.3 The augment pick
At the active terminal, each squadmate independently sees their own 3 augment options. Picks are simultaneous and personal — there's no voting, no friction over who gets what. Four parallel decision moments happen at once.
Each player picks one. The augment is theirs for the rest of the run.
2.4 Augment system
2.4.1 Pool structure
The game contains 300+ total augments. Each map curates a biased pool of approximately 60 augments matching its theme. Mining map = toughness, survivability, weapon-modifiers (e.g. double-shot). Other maps will skew toward other archetypes.
At each terminal, a player rolls 3 augments from the active map's 60-pool. Rolls are independent per player. Overlaps between squadmates are allowed — two players can see and pick the same augment, each gets their own copy.
2.4.2 Augment scope (squad interaction)
Augments are tagged for at-a-glance reading on the pick card:
* Personal — only affects you.
* Aura — affects squadmates within range of you.
* Squad — affects everyone in the squad regardless of position.
* Conditional — triggers on a squad action (revives, kills, etc.).
Suggested visual treatment: colour-coded badge on the augment card. Glance-readable in 2 seconds during a pick.
2.4.3 Tradeoffs (no caps, no "upgrades")
Every augment has a paired downside. There are no stacking caps. The math self-regulates: a player who stacks double-shot 4× has 16× the bullet output but also 16× the recoil, getting catapulted across the map every shot.
This produces several effects:
* Stacking is a real choice, not an automatic upgrade. Every duplicate compounds upside AND downside.
* Builds have shape and personality — a 4× double-shot player is a character ("the maniac"), not a stat block.
* Counter-augments become valuable — recoil dampener, mass boots, knockback resist. The pool self-balances through ecosystem design.
* "Managing your own build's chaos" becomes a core skill expression.
* Hard balance becomes soft balance — players paid for their power with their problems.
2.4.4 Stacking within a player
If a player picks the same augment twice in one run, it stacks. Triple-shot, then quad-shot, then quint-shot. Both upside and downside compound. Picking a duplicate is a real decision: deepen the build's identity, or diversify.
2.4.5 Stacking across players
Two squadmates picking the same augment each get their own personal copy. No squad-level interaction unless the augment is tagged Aura, Squad, or Conditional.
2.5 The shape of a run
Drop → walk to anchor → wave 1 → scout/breathe → terminal lights up → 4 parallel augment picks → wave 2 → ... × ~10 cycles → Mission Control announces threshold → extract or push.
By cycle 6 or 7, the four players don't just have stats — they have personalities. The aura-buff support who can't shoot. The 6× double-shot maniac getting flung around the map. The tank who's so heavy they can't move but everything bounces off them. The glass cannon who one-shots elites but dies to a stiff breeze. Build-as-character is the design payoff.


3. Push-vs-Extract Decision
STATUS: LOCKED
	

The signature decision of the extraction roguelite. Once the squad reaches the map's tech-data threshold (~20 min), they choose: extract with what they've earned, or push deeper for more — knowing pushing means a longer fight back to their pods.
3.1 Map completion
Map completion is announced by Mission Control. Each map has a tech-data threshold (target ~10 terminals). When the threshold is hit, Mission Control radios in:
"The tech data we were looking for has been received. You can stay for additional tech data, or extract — choice is yours."
This is the push-vs-extract gate. There is no hard mechanical wall — the squad continues immediately, but the framing of the choice has now been made explicit by Mission Control's voice. This is the only point in the run where extraction is offered to the squad rather than simply available to them (see 3.6).
Mission Control is the squad's diegetic voice — present every run, persistent across the meta-game. Per-map briefings reinforce map identity. Full character writeup in Section 16.
3.2 Push-deeper changes
When the squad stays past the threshold, two things change, and only two:
3.2.1 Difficulty escalation
Waves continue but the curve sharpens. Tougher enemies, denser spawns, new compositions and spawn behaviours that test parts of the build that base-game waves didn't. The escalation is steady, not a cliff — wave 11 is harder than wave 10, not lethal.
3.2.2 Map state changes (the void reacts)
The environment shifts to reflect that the squad has overstayed. Lighting fails. Ambient sound changes. Enemies emerge from new geography. Crystalline void-growth creeps onto previously-clean surfaces. Mission Control's comms degrade — static, cutouts, professionalism leaking out as the signal weakens. The map the squad has been holding for 20 minutes is no longer the same map.
3.2.3 What does NOT change
The augment pool stays the same map-curated 60. Loot quality stays the same. Terminal cadence stays the same. Push-deeper is not a content gate or reward upgrade — it is a stress test of the build the squad already has. The reward for pushing is finding out how far the build goes. The wipe is part of the point.
3.3 Extraction mechanics
Extraction is geographic, not procedural. Pods exist as part of the map from the moment the squad drops in — four pods at a fixed extraction site, one per player, identified by accent colour. There is no "call extraction" mechanic. Pods are always available; the squad simply has to reach them.
3.3.1 Boarding
A player at their pod can dive in at any time, including mid-wave. Boarding plays a 2–4 second animation: the player dives into the pod, it launches up a tether to the squad leader's ship in orbit. Once launched, that player has extracted with their loot.
3.3.2 Independent fates
Each pod that boards = that player extracts with their loot. Players who don't reach their pod (dead, downed too far, killed during the sprint) lose their loot. The squad does not share a fate at extraction. A run can end with three players home and one dead in the dirt.
3.3.3 No final defensive wave
Extraction is not a separate combat encounter. The "extraction wave" is whichever wave is happening when the squad chooses to leave — the obstacle to your pod is the world's current state, not a scripted final hold.
3.3.4 Squad leader's ship
The pods tether back to a vessel hovering above the map — the squad leader's ship. This is a separate worldbuilding element from Mission Control's console; the squad leader is a separate character/entity (TBD). The ship is the destination; Mission Control is the voice.
3.3.5 Design intent
Extraction is woven into the spatial layer of the run, not bolted on as a closing sequence. Players can see their pod from drop-in. They walk past it between waves. They know exactly how far they are from home at every moment.
3.4 The pull to push
OVERRUN deliberately offers no extrinsic rewards for pushing past completion — no rare loot tier, no exclusive augment pool, no prestige currency. The pull is purely intrinsic.
By the time Mission Control announces completion, each squadmate has spent 20 minutes assembling an absurd, downside-laden build. The build has shape, identity, character. Extracting at wave 10 leaves the question of how far that build goes unanswered. Pushing deeper runs the experiment to its conclusion. Either the build keeps cohering against escalating pressure, or it falls apart spectacularly. Both are satisfying answers. A clean wave-10 extraction is the least satisfying outcome of the three.
3.4.1 Design implications
The choice to push must never be obviously correct. Because the reward is intrinsic (build narrative climax), pushing is only attractive if the squad is invested in their build. Squads with coherent, dangerous builds want to push. Squads with weak builds want to extract clean. The decision is a read on the run, not a min-max calculation.
Different squadmates may want different things. Some want to push; others want out. Combined with independent extraction fates, this creates real conversation under pressure: agreement, disagreement, compromise, sacrifice. The push-vs-extract decision is a squad conversation, not a button press.
Push-deeper waves must test the build, not just inflate numbers. New compositions, new spawn patterns, new spatial pressures — situations the squad's build has to react to, where the build's downsides become exposed. The 6× double-shot player finds out what happens when their flinging-recoil sends them into the void's reach. That exposure of downside is the entire content of "how far does my build go."
Mission Control never advocates for pushing. She announces the choice neutrally, and her tone leans toward recommending extraction as conditions worsen. The pull belongs to the squad alone.
3.5 Pressure to extract
Pressure to extract is primarily geographic. The deeper the squad pushes, the further they are from their pods. Push-deeper is not just temporal pressure (harder waves) — it is spatial commitment. Every meter you advance into the map is a meter you'll have to fight back through to extract.
Combined with the previously locked atmospheric pressures:
* Difficulty escalation (3.2): waves past completion test the build's downsides as well as its upsides. Builds fail under sustained pressure.
* Map state degradation (3.2): lighting fails, void crystallisation spreads, geometry compromises. The path back to the pods is not the same path the squad took out — areas that were safe are now hostile.
* Mission Control's signal degrading: her transmissions worsen as the squad pushes deeper. Static, cutouts, dropouts. By late push-deeper, she may be barely audible. The squad's emotional anchor fades.
* Mission Control's lines themselves: she does not advocate extraction (per 3.4), but her professional surface thins. Concern leaks through. The flickers — usually a reward — become warnings.
* Per-player loot risk (3.3): every wave pushed, each player gambles their personal loot. The further they are from their pod, the more they're gambling.
3.5.1 Design principle
The pressure to extract is a layered atmospheric and geographic pressure, not a timer or meter. Squads decide to extract when they can feel it's time — through their build failing, the world degrading, Mission Control's voice fading, and the growing realisation that getting back to their pod is going to be a fight.
3.6 Early extraction
There is no early extraction as a separate concept. Extraction is always available — the pods exist from drop-in. Any player can sprint for their pod at any time, mid-wave or between, and dive in.
The cost of leaving early is implicit: less contract progress, less loot, less of the run completed. The cost of leaving late is geographic: fighting back through worse waves and a degraded map to reach your pod.
3.6.1 Bad runs have a graceful exit
A squad getting wrecked at wave 4 doesn't have to wipe — they can sprint to their pods and bail. They take home whatever they collected, return to the lobby, queue up again. Wiping costs all run loot; bailing costs only the run's unrealised potential.
3.6.2 Mission Control does not gatekeep
She doesn't approve, deny, or comment on early extraction unprompted. The squad is contractually free to leave at any time — partial delivery is still delivery, partial pay is still pay. Her professional neutrality on this point reinforces the trained-detached surface.
3.6.3 Design intent
Extraction is always a viable choice and never a button-press event. The decision to leave is whatever spatial and tactical commitment the squad is willing to make in that moment to reach their pods. The push-vs-extract choice happens implicitly, continuously, throughout the entire run — but it is amplified at the post-completion announcement, when Mission Control frames the choice explicitly for the only time.

18. Session Start — Lobby Arrival Cinematic
STATUS: LOCKED
	A short cinematic that plays every time the player enters the lobby from the main menu. The opening 30 seconds of every session. Establishes the world, introduces Mission Control’s voice, and seamlessly hands control to the player inside the lobby (which is their jumpship). Most sub-sections below are LOCKED with the open sub-questions tagged.
18.1 Purpose
STATUS: LOCKED
	The cinematic is the first impression of every session. Its job is to (a) establish the lobby as the player’s personal jumpship, (b) introduce Mission Control’s voice as the diegetic narrator of the meta-game, (c) set up the drop tubes as the visible “other contractors enter here” mechanism, and (d) hand off to gameplay seamlessly without a hard cut. It is not a “meet the hero” moment — the player character is deliberately under-characterized, framed only as a slice (shoulder, torso, holstered sidearm).
18.2 Fiction
STATUS: LOCKED
	The player is a contractor. The lobby is their personal jumpship. Drop tubes at the front of the ship are the embarkation point for other contractors who join the session. “Booting up” the ship — slapping the wall plate to initialize the drop tubes — is the diegetic act of signalling to dispatch that this contractor is open for business. Mission Control is dispatch; she works for the contracting corporation, not for the player. Her early-game tone toward the player is procedural and impersonal. “Welcome back, contractor” is how she addresses every contractor on her roster, not a personal greeting.

18.7 LED choreography
STATUS: LOCKED
	The three green status pads at the base of the drop tubes activate sequentially, synchronized to specific phonemes within MC’s line. Each pad fades up rather than snapping on, suggesting systems warming rather than instant electronic response.
Trigger map:
* “Drop” — pad 1 fades on
* “pods” — pad 2 fades on
* “initialized” — pad 3 fades on
The phoneme sync intentionally does not light pads on the verb-stem syllables but on consonant-cluster downbeats, giving the lighting a percussive feel relative to the spoken line.
18.8 Camera handoff
STATUS: LOCKED
	After the wall plate strike, the player character stops moving and stands still for approximately 1 second. During this window, the camera glides smoothly from the static cinematic position to the gameplay 3rd-person spring-arm position behind the player character. The handoff is interpolated, not cut. The transition completes within the 1-second pause.
The player gains full input control at the end of the glide. By default they are facing the drop tubes (toward the front of the ship). The player’s first action — turn around to walk into the lounge, or wait at the tubes — is the first moment of authored gameplay.
A small overlap window exists where the cinematic camera is finishing its glide while input becomes active. This is acceptable; in the rare case the player whips their mouse during the overlap, the resulting view jolt is a discoverable seam, not a bug.
18.9 Tone
STATUS: LOCKED
	Routine. Lived-in. The opening is deliberately not heroic. The player is a contractor doing what they always do — boot up the ship, signal availability to other contractors, wait. The slap of the wall plate is muscle memory, the LED choreography is reflexive, MC’s greeting is corporate. The drama of OVERRUN is what happens later, downstairs in the broken world. The lobby cinematic is the player’s quiet workday beginning.
This tone is mandatory. Future cinematic variants (e.g. once MC’s loyalty arc has progressed) may break this tone; the opening does not.
